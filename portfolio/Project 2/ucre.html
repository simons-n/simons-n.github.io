Art on the Way

User-Centered Research and Evaluation

Fall 2019





Background

My role in this project involved data analysis, creating customer journey maps and stakeholder diagrams, conducting observe and intercept research and think-aloud protocols, creating storyboards and running speed-dating sessions, designing low-fidelity prototypes, and conducting experience prototyping sessions. I shared these responsibilities with fellow students Ari Daly, Irene Lin, and Jamie Dorst.

My specific role was as Quality Assurance Manager, ensuring that our deliverables met the expectations of our professors and client, and upholding visual design and grammar standards.


The Problem Space: Public Art Engagement

For this project, we explored methods by which we could measure engagement with public art, support public art's impacts on livability in Pittsburgh, and justify spending on public art. Our client was Metro21.


Individual Research Phase

The first part of this project involved analyzing research and data collected by a previous cohort that began to tackle this issue, using tools like Tableau and creating customer journey maps and stakeholder diagrams.


Our Focus: Measuring and Increasing Convenient Engagement

My team decided to focus on people who choose to engage with public art only when it is convenient for them to do so, based on insights from our individual research.


Observe and Intercept

We then traversed into the field, observing people in Pittsburgh who we saw near public art, intercepting them to conduct interviews about their experiences. We found that people tend to look at art only when it is convenient for them.

We realized that we wanted to create an intervention that would draw people's attention to public art.




Think-Aloud Protocol

We realized that our proposed intervention has some similarities to online advertisements. Thus, for our think-aloud protocols, we explored the ways in which people interact with ads.

We found that invasive ads annoy users, suggesting to us that our intervention needed to be more passive than invasive.


Storyboards and Speed-Dating

With this in mind, we identified several possible needs, and created multiple storyboards of varying "riskiness" levels for each of those needs.

To validate the needs we identified, we presented the storyboards to participants through speed-dating sessions. We discovered an intriguing opportunity to decrease desensitization to public art.




Experience Prototyping

From there, we decided on a mobile application built to deliver notifications to users when they walked near public art, based on location data, weather, and sentiment extracted from those users' recent social media posts.

We tested this idea through experience prototyping, drawing up representations of screens on paper and asking participants to interact with the system.


Art on the Way

We found that people don't use social media enough for social media sentiment to make sense. Thus, we decided to gain explicit feedback from users upon onboarding about the types of art that they're interested in.

Furthermore, we decided that it would be useful to integrate data from Google Calendar. This will allow the app to deliver notifications to users only when they are free to look at art, and also suggest art for future times.
